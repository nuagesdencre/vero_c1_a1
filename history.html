A glimpse into the Chateau Frontenac's history

William Van Horne, the president of the Canadian Pacific Railway, built the hotel in the late 1800s in hope of attracting rich tourists who would then travel by train. While the hotel was renovated, built upon and suffered a few calamities (fires and tough winters), it still stands strong and picturesque today.

A fortress

The architecture of the Chateau Frontenac is inspired from French medieval castles with an emphasis on Gothic elements. It was designed in 1892 by architect Bruce Price and was first completed in 1893. Many expansions have been added since in a similar style.

The Chateau's Name

As an historical hat tip, the hotel was named after Louis de Buade, Count of Frontenac, who was the Governor General of New France in the late 1600s. This is particularly relevant as Quebec is one of the oldest known settlements in North America, a French-speaking city where a Samuel de Champlain founded a French settlement in 1608; the pride in the French origin of the colonies was indisputed.
